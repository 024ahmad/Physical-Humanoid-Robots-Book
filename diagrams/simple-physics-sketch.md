```
Physics Forces Acting on Humanoid Robot:

1. Weight (W = mg) ↓
   - Acting downward through center of mass
   - Magnitude: Robot mass × gravity

2. Normal Force (N) ↑
   - Ground reaction force perpendicular to surface
   - Equal and opposite to weight in static equilibrium

3. Friction Force (f) →
   - Parallel to surface, opposes motion
   - Magnitude: μ × N (where μ is coefficient of friction)

4. Applied Force (F_app) →
   - Force generated by robot to move forward
   - Must overcome friction to initiate motion

Force Balance Equations:

Vertical: N - W = 0 (no vertical acceleration)
Horizontal: F_app - f = ma (net horizontal force creates acceleration)

Friction Relationships:

Static Friction: f ≤ μ_s × N (prevents motion)
Kinetic Friction: f = μ_k × N (acts during motion)

Where:
- μ_s = coefficient of static friction
- μ_k = coefficient of kinetic friction
- N = normal force
- a = acceleration
- m = robot mass
- g = gravitational acceleration (9.81 m/s²)
```

## Physics Concepts Illustrated

### 1. Force Balance
The diagram shows how forces must balance for stable standing or constant velocity movement. The normal force from the ground balances the robot's weight, while friction opposes or enables forward motion.

### 2. Friction Coefficients
Different surfaces have different friction coefficients:
- Rubber surface: μ_s ≈ 0.8, μ_k ≈ 0.7
- Ice surface: μ_s ≈ 0.1, μ_k ≈ 0.08
- Normal surface: μ_s ≈ 0.6, μ_k ≈ 0.5

### 3. Static vs Kinetic Friction
The robot experiences static friction when stationary or moving without slipping. Once the applied force exceeds the maximum static friction (μ_s × N), the robot begins to slip and experiences kinetic friction.

### 4. Ground Reaction Forces
The ground provides reaction forces that enable the robot to push off and move. These forces must be properly controlled to maintain stability during locomotion.

## Application to Humanoid Locomotion

In humanoid walking:
- The robot shifts its weight to control normal forces
- Friction forces enable forward propulsion
- Proper balance of forces prevents slipping or falling
- Dynamic control adjusts to varying surface conditions

## Significance in Physical AI

This simple physics sketch represents fundamental principles that govern all physical interactions in humanoid robotics. Understanding these forces is essential for creating robots that can move safely and efficiently in the real world.
